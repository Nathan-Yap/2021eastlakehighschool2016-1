Dissolving Brain Biochip

2016-1 Washington TSA State Conference
Discover More

The Innovation

Created by Rory K. J. Murphy et al., the biochip allows doctors to only have to surgically insert a device smaller than the tip of a pencil into the brain. Once inserted, it is hooked up to a wireless transmitter that sends information gathered (such as fluid pressure, temperature, and pH levels) to computers.

Neurosurgeons Wilson Z. “Zack” Ray (left) and Rory K. J. Murphy (right) who worked on creating the dissolvable biochip.

The Dissolving Process

After thirty hours, usually well after the necessary medicine and procedures are administered, the device dissolves by itself, though the exact time depends on the surrounding brain solution.



How it is made

Engineers at the University of Illinois created this using something known as polylactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA), which is an FDA-approved biodegradable and biocompatible acid. For more information on PLGA and its uses, please reference the section below.

The Chip

An example of the biochip dissolving in a mock fluid that is similar to the average composition of brain fluid. Though bits of the SiO2 membrane remain, most of it has completely dissolved.

More Information

A diagram of PLGA. When PLGA comes in contact with HO2 (symbolized by H+, which represents the hydrogen ion in HO2), it decomposes into lactic acid and glycolic acid, two acids naturally found in the body and not harmful unless found in excess.

As mentioned previously, PLGA is the major reason as to why the biochip is biodegradable. After being approved by the FDA, PLGA has been used extensively as a delivery vehicle for drugs, proteins, and macromolecules (most commonly DNA and RNA). An example of PLGA in use is the vancomycin antibiotic, which is used to treat bacterial infections in the bloodstream and brain. In fact, PLGA has been used in the creation of several COVID-19 vaccines!

When PLGA comes in contact with water, it dissolves into lactic acid and glycolic acid. Lactic acid is then metabolized through the tricarboxylic acid cycle (TCA cycle), commonly known as the Krebs cycle which is the process by which cells breaks down organic molecules to harvest energy. Glycolic acid is metabolized the same way before being removed via the kidney.

A picture of Staphylococcus aureus, which is commonly considered the most dangerous of the Staphylococcus bacteria. It received the name aureus due to its golden color (aureus translates to “gold” in Latin).

By using the brain biochip, doctors are able to avoid having to perform surgery to remove the biochip, decreasing chances of infection from diseases such as Staphylococcus aureus. It will also decrease chances of surgical error and medical expenses.

A diagram of the MEG. Currents running through wires will create a magnetic field due to the movement of electrons that can map and calculate important information such as blood flow and intracranial (inside the brain) fluid.

It’s needless to say that there are several other ways to observe the brain other than the aforementioned biochip, but what are they?

The most commonly used one is electroencephalography (EEG), which monitors electrical activity through the scalp. While non-invasive (not requiring surgery), it cannot measure as wide of a range of data like the biochip. Furthermore, costs can skyrocket to as high as $3000 without health insurance. One of the most famous EEG devices, the BIS monitor, costs around $13500 (Canadian dollars), which gives a sense of how much hospitals must charge patients to recover the cost.

Another monitoring device is magnetoencephalography (MEG). Like its name suggests, this device is similar to EEG but instead of using electric signals, it uses magnetic waves. It can detect blood flow, which is essential for doctors, since a decrease in pressure of brain fluid suggests a leak in the meninges (barrier of the brain) which could be fatal if left untreated. MEG can also be used to pinpoint brain activity, which could indicate epilepsy.

Both of these are incredible feats of engineering yet are not enough when it comes to the sheer volume of data surgeons need to ensure the safety of patients.
